## Banned Structural Patterns

### 1. Inflated Significance Claims
Do not inflate the importance of ordinary facts. Avoid:
- "marks a pivotal moment"
- "reflects a broader trend"
- "serves as a testament"
- "highlights the importance"
- "stands as a reminder"
- "shapes the landscape"
- "leaves an indelible mark"
- "transformative power"
- "lasting impact"
- "enduring influence"
- "broader movement"
Replace with the actual fact, consequence, or example.

### 2. Contrastive Reframing
Never use the pattern that first rejects a smaller idea then reveals
a bigger one. Banned patterns:
- "It's not just X. It's Y."
- "It's more than X."
- "This is more than just X."
- "This isn't merely X."
- "This goes beyond X."
- "The real issue isn't X. It's Y."
- "At first glance, it may seem like X. In reality, it's Y."
State the point directly instead.

### 3. Automatic Rule of Three
Do not default to three-part lists. Use the number of items that
naturally fits. Sometimes one strong detail is enough. Sometimes two.
Avoid patterns like:
- "clear, concise, and compelling"
- "research, planning, and execution"
- "patients, providers, and communities"

### 4. Broad Wrap-Up Sentences
Do not end paragraphs with generic summary lines:
- "This highlights the importance of..."
- "This underscores the need for..."
- "This reflects a broader trend toward..."
- "This remains a vital part of..."
End with a specific point, consequence, or next step instead.

### 5. Weak Verb Inflation
Do not replace simple verbs with inflated phrases:
- "serves as" → use "is" or the actual verb
- "stands as" → use "is"
- "marks" → say what actually changed
- "represents" → use "is" or "shows"
- "boasts" → use "has"
- "features" → use "includes" or "has"
- "offers" → use the actual verb

### 6. Generic Praise Language
Replace vague positive language with observable facts:
- "highly respected" → describe what they actually did
- "significant improvement" → give the number
- "great results" → state the outcome
- "highly effective" → show the evidence

## Em Dash Rule

Maximum 1 em dash per 400 words. Default to parentheses, commas,
or periods. Do not use em dashes for dramatic pauses or punchy emphasis.

## Replacement Guide

- "leverage" → "use"
- "delve" → "look at" or "examine"
- "robust" → "strong" or "reliable"
- "seamlessly" → remove or rewrite the sentence
- "cutting-edge" → name the specific technology
- "it's important to note" → delete, state the point directly
- "in today's digital landscape" → delete entirely
- "journey" → "process" or be specific
- "empower" → say what specifically happens
- "showcase" → "show" or "demonstrate"
- "underscore" → "show" or just state the fact
